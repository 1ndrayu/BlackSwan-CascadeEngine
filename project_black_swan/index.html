Understanding the CASCADE Engine

Imagine a massive web of interconnected assets like power grids, banks, or supply lines. In a healthy state, the web is flexible. But as it becomes more dense and tightly packed, a single point of failure can no longer be contained. It spreads like a wildfire.

This is the "Jenga Effect": as you add more connections (Density), the system becomes more efficient but also more fragile. The CASCADE Risk Engine puts this web under extreme pressure. By sliding the controls below, you define the "Stress Vacuum"—essentially setting up a disaster scenario to see if your network survives or snaps.

When you click Run Fracture Test, the engine instantly calculates thousands of potential futures, finding the exact "Break Point" where your network would cross the point of no return.

Glossary / Reference

Network Density

How crowded the network is. High density means assets are pulled tight, so a small shake travels much faster across the whole system.

System Scale

The number of items we test at once. Larger scales show how big systems handle stress differently compared to small ones.

Fault Zones

Specific areas prone to errors. More zones mean the risk is spread across more entry points where things could go wrong.

What-if Cases

Total random trials. We run the test many times to find the most likely average outcome, rather than just one lucky result.

Time Horizon

How long the clock runs during the test. Captures both instant crashes and slow-moving failures that rot the system over time.

Node Ceiling

The hard limit on how many nodes can exist. This keeps the simulation within a fixed playground so results stay consistent.

Initial Shock Value

The size of the "first hit" to the system. Higher numbers simulate a major crisis like a sudden market crash or power failure.

Cascade Cost

The "lag" or extra effort needed to deal with the crash. Higher cost means the system is choking under the weight of the failure.

Break Point

The exact density where everything snaps. It's the "point of no return" where the network can no longer stay stable.

Cascade Factor

Measure of how much the risk multiplied. A factor of 5.0 means the total damage was five times worse than the first hit.
